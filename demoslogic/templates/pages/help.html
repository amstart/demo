Help

On this page, users can map their arguments using QStatements as nodes and Aruments as links in between. When adding an argument, users have to connect statements (which are versions of QStatements) by using them as one of their premises or as conclusion. For example, a QStatement could say: Quantum mechanics is right/wrong. The statements contained here would be Quantum mechanics is right and Quantum mechanics is wrong. On this page, the convention is that the QStatements are represented with the help of question marks, in this case it could be Quantum mechanics is right?

It is possible to voice one's opinion on the matter by voting on which statement is correct. Additionally, it is possible to express what one would view as ideal: Quantum mechanics should be right or Quantum mechanics should be wrong.

The resulting network of linked QStatements can be explored , whereas the QStatements are dark blue and the arguments light blue. The links in between are color-coded: Green means the positive version of the QStatement, red the negative, grey the neutral. A dashed line represents an ideal.
